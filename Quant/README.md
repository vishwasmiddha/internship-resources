# Quant Preparation Guide

You will find very precious advice in the "Senior's Roadmaps" section, which I mostly refer to personally as well. I mostly refer to Avval Amil sir's roadmap for my quant preparation. I will try to cover the things I have learnt in this document.

One thing to note is please do not neglect competitive programming (refer to README in cp folder) while working on quant puzzles, as most company OAs still are programming based, even for quant roles.





## 1\. Getting Started with Quant Preparation

If you're new to Quant preparation and want a taste of what you'll do in the upcoming 2 months of summer, go through some of the [Brainstellar](https://brainstellar.com/) puzzles. I believe this is the most concise (though simple) resource for getting started.





## 2\. Building Intuition with Core Resources

Once you've gone through Some of Brainstellar's problems, What you should do is complete the green book's brain teaser section. Now you'll have a basic intuition for things that appear often in quant puzzles.

One thing I want to mention is that when you are done trying a question and go through the solution, think about every possible variation of the question, why you couldn't get the question, were you close or completely wrong, and by the end you should be absolutely sure that you will always be able to do a similar question in the future. This is especially relevant if you are starting a little late, since you may not have enough time to go through the questions again.

Even if you find a question repeated in a different resource, do it by hand again, do not skip it. This discipline is necessary for mastering quant puzzles.





## 3\. Probability Foundations

Now you'll need some probability background to move on to further puzzles. You can either go through Sheldon Ross(intro to probability), or Prof. Dharmaraja's Book. If you've already done MTL106, you can skip this part. The relevant sections of the book (Prof. Dharma), are -

Link - [Dharmaraja and Sheldon Ross](https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/1so1B9JpMotdSTI5WCGN9J5jgT5bvhq7D)

Ensure that you have a basic understanding of basic probability, conditional probability, how distributions work, and all kinds of random variables. Concepts such as Markov chain, martingale, threshold are also quite useful.

You can also go through [MIT Random variables course](https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-440-probability-and-random-variables-spring-2014/pages/syllabus/) for brief review.





## 4\. Core Quant Resources

Now you should complete all the basic resources before moving on to more diverse questions. Ensure you have completed **Green Book, Fifty challenging problems in probability,** [Twenty challenging problems](https://www.math.ucdavis.edu/~gravner/MAT135A/resources/chpr.pdf), [Brainstellar](https://brainstellar.com/), **Heard on the Street,** [Gurmeet's puzzles](https://gurmeet.net/puzzles/) **(optional)**.

These resources alone can get you very far in your preparation, I recommend you get these finished in at least first half of your summer if you are aiming high, else you may do them till the end of your summer too as they are quite comprehensive.

You can go through [TedEd's yt riddles](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLJicmE8fK0EiFRt1Hm5a_7SJFaikIFW30) in your free time, it is very relevant and fun, so you won't feel tired watching it.

I would also suggest you work on your mental maths daily for 10-15 minutes on [Zetamac](https://arithmetic.zetamac.com/), [Matiks](https://www.matiks.in/) or [Puzzled Quant Mental maths section](https://www.puzzledquant.com/games) (mental maths is especially relevant for Da vinci, optiver). You can also practice on [80 in 8](https://www.80in8.com/) for optiver specific mental maths preparation.







## 5\. Advanced and Diverse Resources

Now you can move on to more diverse resources. What I am currently following is [Quantprof](https://quantprof.org/) , you can also go to [Puzzledquant](https://www.puzzledquant.com/), **quantguide** (in books folder), or Peter winkler book (especially relevant for Graviton). There are 2 Peter winkler books, ensure that you go through the correct one (I've placed it in the Books folder). Pick a resource you find very helpful and dedicate yourself to it completely.

You can also go through "Olympiad Combinatorics - Pranav Sriram" for combinatorics specific hard questions, but I wouldn't recommend spending too much time on it.

I have found the quantprof.org 's Course on their website quite useful if you want all the relevant topics in the form of a roadmap, which are useful for quant prep. (Note that quantprof.org is technically a free resources, as you can ask for a refund within 30 days of purchase, you can do this multiple times if you cannot afford it)

This section concludes most of your quant puzzles preparation.







## 6\. Market Making Skills

Another thing you should work on is your market making skills (obviously, this is secondary to quant puzzles), since this is especially relevant for some companies such as Da vinci. Da vinci's group discussion is a high paced, stressful market making competition. Other companies can also ask you similar stuff.

[Tradermath](https://www.tradermath.org/dashboard) is a great resource which I would highly recommend purchasing for overall market making, numerical tests and interview preparations.

What I do with my friends is open a level 3/4 question on [Quantprof](https://quantprof.org/), set a 1.5 min timer, and solve the question. After the time is up, we quote our bid and ask prices and trade with each other, then we see the answer and compute our PnLs.

I'm working on a [Market Making Website](https://iitdquant.vercel.app/), (inspired by Optiver's tradeathon event) to make market making a more approachable thing for everyone. You are free to play on the website with your friends.

You can also play guesstimates with your friends on this site ([Guesstimates question bank](https://180dcsrcc.in/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Guesstimates-Book-Volume-1-6.pdf) is a source) for fun, though I haven't heard of many companies asking these in their interviews.



Also go through [Trading interview](https://www.tradinginterview.com/quizzes/example-market-making-simulator/) once to get a basic idea of market making workings.



## 7\. Revising Core Mathematics

One common silly mistake people make (myself included) is forget to go over basic maths, such as common integrals and such. Probability and puzzles also have applications of concepts of **Linear Algebra, Integration, Combinatorics, Trignometry, even some basic Number Theory** and other concepts, so do go over them once to refresh your memory.

For Integration, you can refer to Sameer Bansal's book (in Books folder).

I personally haven't found Machine Learning to be very relevant for quant intern prep, though it is highly relevant for competitions and hackathons held by quant companies often. If you have time, get a basic idea of **Regression based models, Gradient boost, Logistic Regression and other such models etc**. so that you have an idea of which model is relevant for what applications, so that you can atleast vibecode for the ongoing quant competitions.

(This is relevant for boosting your CV with hackathons and projects, though if you are in MT1 it may be a good idea to skip this)
Most quants do not look at your CV (only CG, DR, work experience etc.), but ensure you have it completely filled (no unnecessary whitespaces), and have some relevant projects so that you can discuss something in your HR rounds.
